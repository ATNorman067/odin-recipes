BEEF WELLINGTON

A BRIEF HISTORY OF BEEF WELLINGTON

Beef Wellington, an elegant and flavorful dish, carries a name shrouded in mystery. Though it is widely believed to be named after Arthur Wellesley, the first Duke of Wellington, the exact origin of the dish remains unclear. Some suggest it was created to celebrate the Duke's victory at the Battle of Waterloo in 1815, while others believe it was a patriotic twist on the French "filet de boeuf en croûte."

Despite the uncertain history, Beef Wellington's popularity surged in the mid-20th century, thanks in part to its appearance on Julia Child's cooking show. Its combination of tender beef, savory mushroom duxelles, and flaky pastry delighted diners and solidified its place as a fine dining staple. Today, Beef Wellington remains a beloved classic, enjoyed for its exquisite taste and impressive presentation.

BEEF WELLINGTON INGREDIENTS

  • 2 ½ pounds beef tenderloin
  • 4 tablespoons butter, softened, divided
  • 2 tablespoons butter
  • 1 onion, chopped
  • ½ cup sliced fresh mushrooms
  • 2 ounces liver paté
  • salt and pepper to taste
  • 1 (17.5 ounce) package frozen puff pastry, thawed
  • 1 large egg yolk, beaten
  • 1 (10.5 ounce) can beef broth
  • 2 tablespoons red wine

HOW TO MAKE BEEF WELLINGTON

You’ll find the full, step-by-step recipe below – but here’s a brief overview of what you can expect when you make homemade Beef Wellington:

  1. Cook the beef: Cover the beef with butter and bake until browned.
  2. Sauté: Sauté the onions and mushrooms in butter. Mix the paté with butter and seasonings.
  3. Combine: Spread the paté mixture over the beef and top with the veggie mixture.
  4. Roll out pastry: Roll out the pastry dough and place beef in the center.
  5. Wrap it up: Fold up and seal all the edges, place in a baking dish, cut a few slits in the top, and brush with egg yolk.
  6. Bake: Bake according to the instructions until the pastry is golden brown.
  7. Make sauce and serve: Make the red wine sauce and serve alongside the Beef Wellington.
